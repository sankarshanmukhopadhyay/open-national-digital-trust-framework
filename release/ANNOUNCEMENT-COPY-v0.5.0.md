# ONDTF v0.5.0 announcement copy

## GitHub release summary

ONDTF v0.5.0 is the Feature Complete Draft. It establishes the complete planned architectural baseline for governing and assuring national digital trust infrastructure across governance, authority, security, privacy, rights, interoperability, implementation, and standards alignment. The next cycle is implementation-led and will focus on evidence, refinement, deployment experience, and conformance.

## LinkedIn

The Open National Digital Trust Framework has reached v0.5.0, its Feature Complete Draft milestone. ONDTF now provides one coherent, jurisdiction-neutral architecture connecting identity, authority, delegation, policy, evidence, assurance, decisions, effects, accountability, privacy, rights, interoperability, and remedy.

Feature complete does not mean finished. It means the planned foundational domains are now present and integrated. From here, the work shifts from architectural expansion to implementation evidence, interoperability testing, conformance, and external review.

The release includes a complete security and assurance architecture, dedicated data-protection and privacy models, affected-party challenge and remedy, jurisdiction profiling, machine-readable artefacts, a worked reference scenario, and a centrally maintained standards register.

Trust infrastructure should be judged by whether authority can be evaluated, outcomes attributed, evidence tested, harms challenged, and failures remedied. v0.5.0 makes that proposition concrete enough to implement and pressure-test.

## Substack Note

ONDTF v0.5.0 is now a Feature Complete Draft. The milestone matters because the framework no longer treats digital trust as a loose collection of identity, credential, registry, security, and governance components. It connects identity to authority, authority to policy, policy to evidence, evidence to decisions, decisions to effects, and effects to accountability, challenge, and remedy. The next phase is deliberately implementation-led. The question is no longer what foundational domain should be added, but whether independent deployments can make the claims falsifiable, the evidence portable, the controls observable, and the consequences governable.
