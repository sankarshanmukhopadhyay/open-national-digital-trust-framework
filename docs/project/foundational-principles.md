# Foundational principles

These principles provide the stable intellectual and architectural anchor for ONDTF. Profiles and implementations may specialise them, but should not silently weaken or bypass them.

1. **Trust is an operational system property.** Trustworthiness must be expressed through observable controls, evidence, decisions, effects, and accountability rather than institutional assertion alone.
2. **Identity is not authority.** Knowing who or what is acting does not establish what that actor may validly do in a particular context.
3. **Authority must be bounded and attributable.** Delegation, scope, duration, conditions, succession, suspension, and revocation must be explicit and evaluable.
4. **Recognition is governed.** Credentials, registries, attestations, and status information become meaningful only within an identified governance and reliance context.
5. **Assurance is evidence-based and continuous.** A one-time assessment cannot substitute for current evidence, operational monitoring, change control, and reassessment.
6. **Security, privacy, data protection, and rights are distinct but coordinated.** None should be treated as a proxy for the others.
7. **Affected parties matter beyond the principal-agent relationship.** Systems must support transparency, challenge, correction, appeal, remedy, and systemic learning where consequences extend beyond the original delegation.
8. **Interoperability requires semantic and governance alignment.** Syntactic compatibility alone does not establish equivalent meaning, authority, assurance, or legal effect.
9. **Standards are adopted through explicit profiles.** ONDTF records which standards, specifications, and guidelines are selected, constrained, or mapped rather than assuming universal applicability.
10. **Technology neutrality is a design constraint.** The framework does not require a single identifier, credential, registry, protocol, ledger, or vendor stack.
11. **Failure must be safe, visible, and recoverable.** Uncertainty, stale evidence, revocation lag, degraded dependencies, and policy conflict must produce governed outcomes rather than silent continuation.
12. **Conformance claims must be scoped and falsifiable.** Every claim should identify the applicable version, profile, capability, evidence, and assessment boundary.
